\begin{abstract}
We report the results of a new parameter study of \small{STARFORGE} simulations of giant molecular clouds covering 2 decades in both cloud surface density $\Sigma$ and metallicity $Z$, intended to model the emergence of stellar populations across a broad range of galactic environments, including dense starburst conditions. We demonstrate high ($\gtrsim 20\%$) star formation efficiency at high $\Sigma$, but not as high as previous models assuming a constant IMF, because the rate of feedback per stellar mass also increases with $\Sigma$. The IMF peak mass, upper mass limit, and log-normal variance all vary across parameter space; only the high-mass slope has limited variation. We disentangle the processes regulating the formation of the most massive stars, finding that both infrared radiation pressure on dust grains and stellar winds help regulate the maximum stellar mass to the observed value on the order of $\sim 100 M_\odot$ in the local Universe. But at low ($0.01-0.1Z_\odot$) metallicity, both winds and radiation pressure are much weaker, and more-massive stars can form: one of our $0.01 Z_\odot$ models forms several extremely-massive stars (EMS; $10^3+ M_\odot$) as massive as $1750 M_\odot$ through accretion. In this simulation, 4 EMS in a quadruple accrete half the mass of the entire star cluster, resulting in exotic stellar population properties. Our results suggest that stellar populations formed in high-density, metal-poor starburst conditions would have had a significantly elevated stellar mass limit, possibly including EMS.
\keywords{stars: mass function -- galaxies: star clusters: general -- stars: formation }
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Most of what is known about star formation (SF) comes from studying nearby systems. The initial conditions of star formation in our Galaxy are well-characterized by high-resolution maps traced by dust \citep{gutermuth:2009.dust.extinction, Andre_2010_filaments,lombardi:2010.sigma.gmc,heyer:2016.clumps} and molecular transitions \citep{heyer:2009.gmcs,kauffmann:2017.hcn,miville:2017.gmcs} in nearby molecular clouds.  One can also infer key properties of young stellar populations, such as the rate and efficiency of star formation \citep{heyer:2016.clumps,pokhrel:2021.gmc.sfe}, the stellar initial mass function (IMF; \citealt{Hopkins_A_2018_IMF_obs_review}) and multiplicity \citep{offner:2023.multiples.review}, all the way down to the smallest stellar masses \citep[e.g.][]{kirkpatrick:2023.browndwarf.imf}. Many questions about the details of SF remain, but these data constrain the space of viable models for SF in our Galaxy \citep{mckee:2007.review,krumholz:2014.review}

Not all stars form in such readily-observed conditions. Dense, massive globular clusters linger as the relics of an ancient mode of star formation that must have taken place at high density and low metallicity. The Galactic center hosts gas densities and pressures orders of magntiude higher than the Solar neighborhood, and the Milky Way's most massive young stars and star clusters are found there \citep{2016A&A...595A..27G,2019ApJ...870...44H}. Just beyond, in the LMC, high-pressure, low-metallicity gas conditions have produced the Local Group's most massive young star cluster R136, which also hosts the massive known stars \citep{Crowther_most_massive_stars_R136_2016}. And further still are the luminous infrared galaxies (LIRG), where stars form rapidly at even higher intensities \citep{kennicutt:1998.review}.

In a cosmic sense, such ``extreme" star formation is not exceptional: most of the stars that will ever form have already formed around ``cosmic noon" at $z\sim 2$ \citep{2014ARA&A..52..415M}, when galaxies typically were more gas-rich and hosted high gas surface densities and pressures \citep{Swinbank_2011_dense_galaxy_ISM, 2019NatAs...3.1115D,tacconi:2020.cosmic.ism,2020ARA&A..58..661F}. At the even earlier epoch of ``cosmic dawn", the James Webb Space Telescope has detected extremely rest-UV-bright, compact sources suggesting that star formation proceeded very rapidly and at high densities \citep{robertson:2023.hiz.galaxies,labbe:2023.hiz.candidates,2024ApJ...964..150D}. Galaxy luminosities and/or SFRs at cosmic dawn were not generally anticipated by models, and possible sources of discrepancy may include unaccounted-for star formation or feedback dynamics \citep{2023ApJ...955L..35S,dekel:2023.ffb}, errors in the cosmological model \citep{boylankolchin:2023.does.webb.break.lcdm}, or an unusual IMF with greater abundance or mass limit of massive stars \citep{cameron:2023.jwst.topheavy.imf,vink:2023.jwst.nitrogen.vms}.

Recently it has become possible to model the emergence of stellar populations from physical processes in different environments with a greater fidelity and self-consistency. The {\small STARFORGE}\footnote{\url{https://starforge.space}} model simulates star formation from initial gas collapse to cloud disruption, combining a wide range star formation physics into a single numerical model, notably accounting for all forms of stellar feedback: protostellar outflows, radiation, winds, and supernovae \citep{starforge.methods}. This has proven essential to obtaining realistic results, because the various feedback processes must act in concert to simultaneously regulate both low-mass \citep{wang_2010_jets,Cunningham_2011_outflow_sim,hansen12a,mathew:2021.imf.jets,starforge_jets_imf,lebreuilly:2024.jets.sf} and high-mass star formation \citep{larson:1971.masslimit,wolfire:1987.massive.sf,kuiper_2010_massive_sf,krumholz:2009.massive.sf,rosen:2016.massive.sf,rosen_2020_jets_radiation,rosen_2021_winds,guszejnov:2022.starforge.imf}, while also regulating the {\it overall} star formation rate to the low cloud-scale efficiencies seen in the Milky Way (\citealt{geen:2017, grudic:2018.gmc.sfe, kim:2018.uv.feedback,evans:2021.virial.sfe,starforge.fullphysics}, see \citealt{chevance:2023.gmc.review} for review).

In this work we apply the {\small STARFORGE} model to the higher-density conditions than previous work, targeting a mean cloud-scale surface density of $\Sigma = M/\uppi R^2 \approx 1 \rm g\,cm^{-2}$ across a range of cloud masses and metallicities. We focus on global cloud and star cluster evolution and the final stellar population properties, in particular highlighting how these can differ from the more Milky Way-like conditions we modeled in previous works. 

\section{Methods}
We perform a suite of numerical radiation-magnetohydrodynamics simulations of giant molecular clouds using the \small{STARFORGE} numerical framework implemented in the \small{GIZMO} code \citep{hopkins:gizmo}. Here we only briefly summarize the elements of this framework, and refer to the \small{STARFORGE} methods paper \citet{starforge.methods} for details.

We solve the equations of ideal magnetohydrodynamics using \small{GIZMO}'s quasi-Lagrangian, meshless-finite-mass (MFM) Godunov-type solver \citep{hopkins:gizmo.mhd}, with \citep{powell1999} and \citep{dedner2002} divergence-cleaning source terms, and using the \citet{hopkins:gizmo.mhd.cg} constrained-gradient scheme to minimize $\mathbf{\nabla} \cdot \mathbf{B}$. In all simulations presented here, the fluid is discretized into finite-mass elements initially of mass $10^{-3}M_\odot$, resolving a $2\times 10^4 M_\odot$ cloud into $2\times 10^7$ elements. We have shown this resolution to be sufficient to model the IMF down to a completeness limit of $\sim 0.1 M_\odot$ \citep{starforge.methods}.

Gas elements are converted to sink particles once a host of criteria designed to identify centers of unresolved collapse are satisfied, listed fully in \citet{starforge.methods}. Subsequently, sink particles interact via gas accretion, gravity, and stellar feedback, using the \citet{Offner_2009_radiative_sim} semianalytic protostellar evolution model to determine the properties of the (proto-) star as it accretes and evolves.

Self-gravity for gas is solved with a conservative, fully-adaptive force softening scheme \citep{price_2007_adaptive_softening,hopkins:2023.adaptive.softening}, giving consistency between the mass discretization represented in the MHD solver and the gravity solver to within truncation error. Sink particles use a fixed softening radius of $18 \rm AU$. Gas cells call the gravity solver only as frequently as required to capture gravitational dynamics, according to the \citet{grudic:2021.adaptive} lazy force evaluation scheme that, in practice making the cost of gravity a very small component of the overall simulation cost.

We model the thermal evolution of the ISM accounting for the heating and cooling of a host of ionic, atomic, molecular, dust, radiative, and cosmic ray processes, as described in \citet{fire3}. The abundance $\rm H_2$ is evolved according to a reduced chemical network that we Strang-split with the implicit cooling and ionization equilibrium solution step.

We account for stellar feedback from protostellar jets, radiation, winds, and supernovae. We solve the radiation-hydrodynamics equations in 5 photon energy bins: H-ionizing extreme-UV, far-UV, near-UV, optical and near-IR, and the far-IR. We adopt the \citet{levermore_1984_M1} $\rm M_1$ moments closure and the reduced speed of light approximation \citep[e.g.][]{rosdahl:2013.ramses.rt,skinner:2013.hyperion}.

\subsection{Updates to the STARFORGE model}
The \small{STARFORGE} simulations have been in continuous development since the original model was presented in \citet{starforge.methods} and first deployed in \citet{starforge.fullphysics}. Various incremental improvements that have been made since \citet{starforge.methods}, and have been incorporated into the present simulations.
\begin{itemize}
\item We have increased the reduced speed of light from $30\,\rm km\,s^{-1}$ to $90\,\rm km\,s^{-1}$ and ensure that photons colocated with accreted gas cells are promptly re-emitted by the accreting sink particle. These changes matter mainly for capturing the radiation field and temperature profiles in the optically-thick accreting envelopes of massive stars. This choice unfortunately makes the simulations $3 \times$ more expensive to run, and only partially mitigates the fundamental issue that the effective optically-thick transport speed of photons $\tilde{c}/\tau$ becomes smaller than the flow speed near massive stars, causing them to be advected inward in an unphysical dynamic-diffusion regime.
\item Direct force summation is used for all star-star gravity interactions at distances $<1000 \rm AU$, improving general accuracy and conservation in small-$N$ body encounters.
\item The dust temperature is computed by root-finding the stationary dust heat equation including radiative emission, absorption, and gas-dust collisions \citep{hollenbach:1979.molecule.formation.shocks}. Before, the gas-dust term was treated as a term in the gas heat equation but ignored in the dust heat equation, generally resulting in slightly underestimated temperatures overall and a lack of self-consistency in the thermal evolution of dense, shielded gas.
\item Instead of assuming a constant cosmic-ray ionization rate throughout the cloud, we account for the attenuation of cosmic rays similarly to \citet{kim:2022.athena.ism.physics}:
\begin{equation}
\zeta_{\rm CR} = \zeta_{\rm CR,0}\min\left[1, \left(\frac{N_{\rm H}}{10^{21}\,\rm cm^{-2}}\right)^{-1} \exp\left(-\frac{N_{\rm H}}{6 \times 10^{25}\,\rm cm^{-2}}\right)\right],
\end{equation}
where $N_{\rm H}=n_{\rm H} \left(\left(\Delta m/\rho\right)^{1/3} + \frac{\rho}{|| \nabla \rho||} \right)$ is the local scale-length estimator of the column density of H nuclei. Note that we have added an exponential cutoff at extremely high ($\gtrsim 100\,\rm g\,\rm cm^{-2}$) column densities. We default to the Solar-neighborhood value $\zeta_{\rm CR,0}=1.6\times 10^{-17}\,\rm s^{-1}$ \citep{cummings:2016.cosmic.rays.voyager}, and scale it to different conditions as described in \ref{sec:isrf}.
\end{itemize}
\subsubsection{Stellar winds}

In our preliminary experiments it was immediately apparent that more-massive stars would form than we had previously seen at high $\Sigma$, so the wind mass-loss rate for very massive stars needed to be considered more carefully. We emphasize that the actual rates remain quite uncertain \citep{smith_2014_stellarwinds}, particularly for very massive stars. However, for very massive stars it is generally predicted that the mass-loss rate should eventually scale more steeply than the main-sequence O-star relation for metal-line driven winds. To model this, we incorporate the  ``$L/M$-dependent'' mass-loss prescription presented in \citet{sabhahit:2022.vms.mass.loss}, which they argue reproduces the observed narrow range of $T_{\rm eff}$ seen in 30 Doradus in the VLT-flames survey.

\subsubsection{Environmental scaling of background radiation and cosmic rays} 
\label{sec:isrf}
When surveying cloud conditions at different surface densities, we attempt to scale the ambient cosmic ray and interstellar radiation backgrounds to levels appropriate for the environments those clouds would be found in. For the low-$\Sigma$ clouds resembling those found in Milky Way conditions, we adopt standard Solar neighborhood values for the interstellar radiation field (ISRF) across our 5 photon energy bins \citep{draine_1978_isrf,kim:2022.athena.ism.physics}. Then we assume that the ISRF is powered primarily by young stars, and hence scales $\propto \Sigma_{\rm SFR}$ in the region of the galaxy in which the cloud is embedded. To relate $\Sigma_{\rm SFR}$ to the cloud-scale $\Sigma$, we assume a Kennicutt-Schimdt-type scaling $\Sigma_{\rm SFR} \propto \Sigma^2$\footnote{This is steeper than e.g. the \citet{kennicutt:1998.review} galaxy-scale relation $\Sigma_{\rm SFR}\propto \Sigma_{\rm gas}^{1.4}$; on the other hand, the spatial correlation of star formation may lead to a clumping enhancement for the effective local $\Sigma_{\rm SFR}$ and radiation field. Ultimately the scaling estimate should be considered uncertain.}, and thus we scale the ISRF by a factor of $10^4$ when we scale $\Sigma$ $\times 10^2$. We realize the ISRF in the simulation by fixing the net radiative flux to 0 and radiation energy density to the fixed ISRF SED in near the boundary of the simulation domain.

For the cosmic ray ionization rate, we assume that cosmic ray losses within the galaxy are calorimetric, and hence $\zeta_{\rm CR} \propto \tau_{\rm dep}^{-1} = \Sigma_{\rm SFR} / \Sigma_{\rm gas}$. For us this gives $\zeta_{\rm CR,0} \propto \Sigma$, i.e. we scale the cosmic ray ionization $\times 100$ when we scale $\Sigma \times 100$. 


\subsection{Initial conditions}
\input{tables/run_table.tex}

To initialize the simulations from a self-consistent turbulent state, we use the \small{TURBSPHERE} initial conditions generation procedure from \citet{lane:2021.turbsphere}. First, we initialize a standard ``Sphere'' setup consisting of a uniform-density sphere surrounded by a $1000\times$-more-diffuse medium, with an initial Gaussian random velocity field scaled so that the initial virial parameter is 2 and the initial magnetic energy within the cloud is $10\%$ of the turbulent energy. 

We then apply a turbulent driving force realized as an Ornstein-\"{U}hlenbeck process in Fourier space \citep{federrath:2008.density.pdf.vs.forcingtype} with purely-solenoidal forcing, and evolve the cloud with this forcing while confining it to the center of the box with a soft, logarithmic artificial potential. For all models this initial turbulent stirring phase lasts $5t_{\rm ff}$, where $t_{\rm ff}=\pi\sqrt{R^3/8GM}$ is the initial free-fall time of the cloud at its initial uniform density. As in \citet{lane:2021.turbsphere}, the parameters of the confining potential and the driving force's modes and amplitude have been tuned to roughly preserve the initial half-mass radius of the cloud and to fluctuate around a turbulent virial parameter $\alpha \sim 1$ in a steady state.

\subsection{Study design}
Our parameter study covers 2 decades in surface density and metallicity. We fix the cloud mass at $2\times 10^{4}M_\odot$ and consider two surface densities. At the low-density end, we take $R_{\rm cloud}=10\rm pc$ and hence $\Sigma = M_{\rm cloud}/\pi R_{\rm cloud}^2 = 64M_\odot\,\rm pc^{-2}$, which is representative of GMCs in present-day local galaxies; \citep{heyer:2015.gmcs,lada:2020.sigma.gmc.const}. At the high-density end $\Sigma=6300M_\odot\,\rm pc^{-2}$ (which would model more-extreme star-forming conditions such as the Galactic center \citep{Longmore_2012_the_Brick}, local starbursts \citep{mathews:2018.antennae.sfe}). For our low-$\Sigma$ clouds we have multiple statistical realizations from different random seeds for the initial turbulent velocities and turbulent driving: 3 at $0.01Z_\odot$ and 8 at $Z_\odot$. We summarize the physical properties of our grid of simulated clouds in Table \ref{table:runs}.

In addition, to help distentangle which processes regulate massive star formation, we re-simulate our high-$\Sigma$, high-$Z$ with 3 additional variations in the physics model: disabling infrared radiation pressure on dust grains, disabling stellar winds, and increasing the mass-loss rate of stellar winds by a factor of 3 (bringing the prescription into better agreement with e.g. \citet{vink:2001.ob.mass.loss}).

The simulations all follow a similar sequence of star formation as has been documented our prior simulations \citep{starforge.fullphysics,guszejnov:2022.starforge.imf}: from the initial gas-only state, core collapse and star formation are seeded through turbulent collapse and subsequently fed by ongoing accretion, until the force of stellar feedback inevitably becomes sufficient to disrupt the cloud entirely, halting the star formation and leaving behind a gas-free star cluster. Figure \ref{fig:multipanel} shows a time-lapse of various gas properties and the evolution of the star cluster in our most-extreme model \texttt{M2e4\_R1\_Z0.01}.


\subsection{Star formation history and efficiency}

	\label{fig:sfe_vs_time}
\end{figure}


\begin{figure}
	\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/sfe_vs_sigma.pdf}
	\caption{Final star formation efficiency as a function of the initial cloud surface density (points are jittered randomly along the $\Sigma$ axis for visibility). For comparison we plot the relation fit to the \citet{grudic_2020_cluster_formation} simulations and their scatter, and a power-law fit to the present Solar metallicity simulations with standard physics.}
	\label{fig:sfe}

In Figure~\ref{fig:sfe_vs_time} we plot the integrated star formation efficiency versus time, scaling time to the free-fall time. As in prior simulations, we see fairly steep ramp in the stellar mass at early times, predominantly close to $\propto t^3$ \citep{guszejnov:2022.starforge.imf}. As shown by \citet{lane:2021.turbsphere}, the \small{TURBSPHERE} initial configuration does not behave substantially differently from the prior \small{SPHERE} models in this regard. As in prior simulations \citep{grudic:2016.sfe,kim:2018.uv.feedback}, the star formation sequence lasts approximately $\sim 1 t_{\rm ff}$, across more than an order of magnitude in the particular value of $t_{\rm ff}$ \citep[e.g.][]{elmegreen:2000}.

Once a sufficient number of massive stars has formed, stellar feedback becomes sufficient to disrupt the cloud with an HII region or stellar wind bubble, and shuts down star formation. As in \citet{starforge.fullphysics}, SNe do occur in some of the \texttt{M2e4\_R10} runs, but generally too late to influence the initial star formation episode. We report the final SFEs in Table \ref{table:integrated_props}, and plot them in Fig. \ref{fig:sfe} as a function of $\Sigma$, which numerous prior models have found to be the primary cloud property determining its SFE \citep{fall:2010.sf.eff.vs.surfacedensity, geen:2017, kim:2018.uv.feedback,grudic:2016.sfe,he_2019_gmc_fb,fukushima:2021.gmc.rhd.sim, polak:2024.sfe}. 

Unsurprisingly, the SFE does scale with $\Sigma$ in our simulations. However, the scaling is clearly not as steep as e.g. the asymptotic $\propto \Sigma$ scaling predicted by \citet{fall:2010.sf.eff.vs.surfacedensity} for simple momentum feedback, which did fit the previous generation of \small{GIZMO} GMC simulations \citep{grudic:2016.sfe,grudic:2018.gmc.sfe,grudic_2020_cluster_formation}. An unweighted least-squares log-space fit of the Solar metallicity points with standard physics gives $\rm SFE \propto \Sigma^{0.4}$. 

This is probably due to the variation in the specific feedback rate per stellar mass. Revisiting the original \citet{fall:2010.sf.eff.vs.surfacedensity} argument for momentum feedback, but assuming a $\Sigma$-dependent specific momentum rate
\begin{equation}
	\langle \dot{P}/M_\star\rangle = \langle \dot{P}/M_\star\rangle_0 \left(\frac{\Sigma}{\Sigma_0}\right)^k,
\end{equation}
the balance of momentum injection and gravity in the cloud for $\epsilon << 1$ reads

\begin{equation}
	\frac{GM^2}{R^2} \propto \langle \dot{P}/M_\star\rangle_0 \left(\frac{\Sigma}{\Sigma_0}\right)^k \epsilon M,
\end{equation}
which rearranges to
\begin{equation}
	\epsilon \propto \Sigma^{1-k}.
\end{equation}
For the simple case of optically-thick single-scattered photon momentum $\dot{P}/M_\star = L/M_\star/c$, and we see from \ref{table:integrated_props} that $L/M_\star$ scales by an order-of-magnitude over 2 decades in $\Sigma$, so $k \sim 0.5$ and $\epsilon \propto \Sigma^{0.5}$, close to the simulation result.

The scaling of SFE with $Z$ is more subtle. At low $\Sigma$, no clear scaling is discernible. At high $\Sigma$, SFE increases by a factor of $\sim 2$ over 2 decades in $Z$. This is likely due to the lesser intrinsic strength of radiation pressure and stellar wind feedback at low metallicity. Both effects are clearly relevant at $Z_\odot$: strengthening and removing wind feedback noticeably decrease and increase the SFE respectively, and disabling infrared radiation pressure on dust grains increases SFE.

We measure the simulated stellar initial mass functions by tracking the highest mass that each star in the simulation even attains and assuming that to be ZAMS mass. We fit the IMFs to a modified \citet{chabrier_review_2003} form with a variable high-mass slope $\Gamma$, peak mass $m_{\rm c}$, log-normal variance in dex $\sigma$, and hard cutoff mass $M_{\rm max}$, by performing Monte Carlo sampling of the posterior parameter distribution given the data. We detail the sampling procedure fully in Appendix \ref{appendix:mcmc}. We consider only stars $>0.1M_\odot$ in our sample, which we take as our lower completeness limit due to numerical resolution \citep{starforge.methods}; these simulations do not have sufficient resolution to model brown dwarfs. For the cloud parameters with multiple realizations, we plot and fit the aggregate sample as in e.g. \citep{grudic:2023.does.god.play.dice}.

The IMF histograms and fits are plotted in Figure \ref{fig:imf}, and we summarize the IMF posterior parameter distributions of the different simulations are summarized in Table \ref{table:imf_params}. In general, the IMF parameters vary with the cloud parameters. The peak mass $m_{\rm c}$ is $\sim 0.25 M_\odot$ in the Milky Way-like \texttt{M2e4\_R10\_Z1} clouds, close to the \citet{chabrier_imf} value. In all other conditions, $m_{\rm c}$ is greater, significantly so at $>1M_\odot$ for \texttt{M2e4\_R10\_Z0.01}. The variance $\sigma$ agrees best with the \citet{chabrier_imf} value for the \texttt{M2e4\_R1} runs; the IMF is narrower for \texttt{M2e4\_R10\_Z1} and broader for \texttt{M2e4\_R10\_Z0.01}. The high-mass slope is not well-constrained for \texttt{M2e4\_R10\_Z0.01} because the log-normal component fits most of the mass range, but in all other models there is a well-constrained high-mass slope in the $-0.7$ to $-0.8$ range, compared the \citet{salpeter_slope} value of $-1.35$ adopted by \citet{chabrier_imf}. We place these IMFs in context of existing observations in Figure \ref{fig:alphaplot}, and discuss the comparison further in \S\ref{sec:imf.comparison}.

The IMF of \texttt{M2e4\_R1\_Z0.01} exhibits a clear departure from a single-component model: there is a mass gap between $116 M_\odot$ and $981M_\odot$. Assuming the maximum-likelihood Chabrier fit given in Table \ref{table:imf_params}, we would expect $\int_{m_1}^{m_2}\phi\left(\log m\right)\,\mathrm{d}\log m = 0.7\%$ of stars to lie in this gap, or 7.6 on average for this sample size, and the likelihood of drawing none is $5\times 10^{-4}$. Consistently, in Figure \ref{fig:lognormal} we compare the single-component Chabrier fit with a two-component mixture with a Gaussian to model the high-mass excess, finding that the 2-component model has $\Delta \ln \mathcal{L} \sim 10$. Per the Wilks likelihood-ratio test for embedded models, this disfavors the single-component model with $p \sim 3 \times 10^{-4}$. The four $\sim 10^{3} M_\odot$ stars formed in this simulation can be considered a separate population, and a two-component model is most appropriate for describing the distribution. This is a qualitatively-different result than has been seen in any prior \texttt{STARFORGE} simulation; a seemingly similar result was found by \citet{chon:2020.sms.formation}, but for very low ($\lesssim 10^{-3}Z_\odot$) conditions. We will analyze this mode of star formation in detail in subsequent sections.

The most dramatic variation in the simulated IMF is in the maximum stellar mass. We plot the sample-maximum stellar mass of the different simulations as a function of total cluster mass in Figure \ref{fig:mmax}, compared with the observations compiled by \citet{yan:2023.mmax.mecl.relation}, and including multiple indepdendent measurements of the most massive-known star R136a1 to give a sense of the systematic uncertainties (see discussion in caption). 

The low-$\Sigma$ clouds have similar $M_{\rm max} \sim 33-37 M_\odot$, regardless of the metallicity, but at high $\Sigma$ $M_{\rm max}$ scales from $\sim 200 M_\odot$ at $Z_\odot$ to nearly $2000M_\odot$ at $0.01Z_\odot$. The physics experiments provide some insight as to why such massive stars can form at low metallicity: disabling {\it either} stellar winds or IR radiation pressure on dust grains at $Z_\odot$ causes $M_{\rm max}$ to increase, so both processes are responsible for regulating the growth of the most massive stars. Both processes are significantly weaker at low $Z$: $\propto Z$ for dust radiation pressure and $\propto Z_\odot^{0.7}$ for winds. Without them, runaway accretion can proceed unabated, as in \citet{chon:2020.sms.formation}; we will now characterize this process further.

To conclude our analysis, we document the formation of 4 extremely-massive\footnote{We adopt the terminology used in \citet{gieles:2025.inertial.inflow.ems}: $10^2-10^3M_\odot$: Very Massive Stars (VMS). $10^3-10^4M_\odot$: Extremely Massive Stars (EMS). $10^4+ M_\odot$: Supermassive Stars (SMS).} stars in \texttt{M2e4\_R1\_Z0.01}. Figure \ref{fig:ems_accretion_history} plots accretion history of the 10 most-massive stars in this simulation. Stars 1-3 all initially form quite early, within $10\,\rm kyr$ of the first star; star 4 forms roughly $30 \rm\, kyr$ later. The proto-EMS grow as they accrete more and more rapidly, with their accretion rates ramping up in conjunction with that of the cluster as a whole. 

When they are roughly $100M_\odot$, stars 1 and 2 experience a major accretion episode around $75 \rm kyr$, during which they dominate the accretion rate of the cluster as a whole. Accretion continues, and eventually the 4 stars once again account for most of the accretion overall. The accretion rate increases even more, reaching a rate comparable to $M_{\rm cloud}/t_{\rm ff}$: in effect the entire remainder of the cloud has converged into a monolithic accretion flow. Finally, the mechanical effect of wind feedback from the EMS becomes great enough that it reverses the accretion flow. This marks the end of accretion in general, and the star cluster transitions to net mass-loss amidst a diffuse, hot stellar wind bubble (e.g. the fourth column of Fig. \ref{fig:multipanel}).

From Figure \ref{fig:ems_accretion_rate} it is evident that the accretion rates of the 4 EMS are highly correlated. This is because these stars eventually end up in a {\it quadruple system} that accretes as one. Figure \ref{fig:ems_distance_from_center} plots the distances of the stars from the center of mass of the 4-star system. The quadruple pairs up hierarchically: first stars 1 and 2 pair up, and likewise for stars 3 and 4. Then, the system makes its first pericenter passage at $\sim 80 \rm kyr$, after which it becomes gravitationally bound, and the system hardens through dissipation in repeated passages with increasing frequency. Eventually the closest passages are comparable to the $18 \rm AU$ softening scale, and the evolution of the multiple system cannot no longer be followed reliably \citep[e.g.][]{guszejnov:2023.starforge.multiplicity}.

To distinguish between the different scalings predicted by different scenarios for massive star formation, \ref{fig:ems_accretion_rate} plots the accretion rate as a function of stellar mass. Of the various physically-motivated scaling laws, the best fit is $\dot{M} \propto M^{2/3}$. This corresponds to the regime of competitive accretion wherein the stars accretion from a common cluster-scale inflow and accrete with a capture cross-section proportional to the size of their Roche lobe in the cluster environment \citep{bonnell:2001.competitive.accretion}. The scenario proposed in that work appears to be a fair description of our simulation result.


\section{Discussion}
\subsection{Star formation efficiency}
Our simulations predict a scaling of the cloud-scale star formation efficiency across 2 dex in surface density and metallicity, for the first time in a model with self-consistent individually-forming and accreting stars and all stellar feedback processes. Our results largely confirm the prior analytic arguments \citep{fall:2010.sf.eff.vs.surfacedensity,murray:2010.sfe.mw.gmc} and numerical simulations coupling feedback without self-consistent star formation \citep{geen:2017, kim:2018.uv.feedback,grudic:2016.sfe}. Quite generally and inevitably, a higher SFE is required for feedback to be effective in a denser ISM, regardless of the particular mechanism or configuration. 

The key area where we diverge from prior works is that the specific rates of feedback from our stellar populations are not universal, due to the variation in the IMF that emerges from the simulations (Fig \ref{fig:imf}). Our higher-density and lower-metallicity clouds will generally produce more-massive stars. Because the overall feedback budget is dominated by these high-mass stars, the specific feedback rate of the stellar population as a whole can be much greater.

We caution that direct, statistically-robust observational evidence for such a scaling within a homogeneous dataset has yet to be observed \footnote{\citet{mok:2020} note general agreement between observed star cluster masses and those predicted from observed GMC masses by the \citet{fall:2010.sf.eff.vs.surfacedensity} SFE model, and likewise \citet{grudic_2020_cluster_formation} for simulation predictions, but neither demonstrates direct evidence for a {\it scaling} in SFE within a given dataset.}, and in fact the cloud-scale {\it per-freefall} SFE has been shown to {\it anticorrelate} with $\Sigma$ in nearby galaxies in the PHANGS sample \citep{leroy:2025.phangs.sfe}. It is unclear whether this represents a true tension between models and observations, or whether the observations may be probing a different notion of SFE than we are measuring from the simulations.

\subsection{The simulated and observed IMF across different conditions}
\label{sec:imf.comparison}
Figure \ref{fig:alphaplot} is the most comprehensive comparison to date made between observations and simulations of the IMF, in terms of the breath of the dataset considered, range of stellar masses, and parameter space covered. We take this opportunity to comment on the general level of agreement between current models and observations.

First, we note that the \small{STARFORGE} high-mass IMF slopes range from $\sim -0.7$ to $-0.8$ (whereas the \citet{salpeter_slope} slope here would be $-1.35$), with the sole exception of the low-density, low-metallicity \texttt{M2e4\_R10\_Z0.01}, whose asymptotic high-mass slope is not well-constrained. These values are consistent with some with observations: similar high-mass slopes have been inferred in observed star clusters, predominantly in star clusters located near the Galactic center \citep{brandner:2008.westerlund.imf,hussmann:2012.quintuplet.imf, hosek:2019.arches.imf} and 30 Doradus \citep{schneider:2018.30dor.imf.excess}. Our \texttt{M2e4\_R1\_Z1} simulation would be the best model for the conditions those clusters would have formed in, and the level of agreement in both the maximum stellar mass and the high-mass slope is excellent. \citet{hosek:2019.arches.imf} reported a degeneracy between an IMF with a break at $\sim 5M_\odot$ and an IMF with a more-conventional low-to-intermediate mass shape: if we take \texttt{M2e4\_R1\_Z1} as a physically-informed prior, it would lend weight to the latter, more conventionally-shaped IMF.

On the other hand, it should not be dismissed that the present simulation suite does not have any examples of star clusters with high-mass slopes close to or steeper the \citet{salpeter_slope}, where most of the observations do lie, predominantly in Milky Way disk conditions that would be most closely approximated by \small{M2e4\_R10\_Z1}. Individual OB associations formed in such conditions with slopes consistent with this have been reported \citep{massey:1995.mw.ob.assoc.imf}, but are the exception and not the rule. Meanwhile, measurements of the high-mass slope in young clusters in M31 and M33 are arguably the cleanest homogeneous dataset to date, and they are generally statistically consistent with a single slope of $-1.5$ \citep{weisz:2015.m31.imf, wainer:2024.m33.imf}.

The \small{STARFORGE} IMF has in fact become shallower since \citet{starforge.fullphysics}, which predicted $\Gamma \sim -1$, despite many bugfixes and improvements to the realism of the physics. Our introduction of \small{TURBSPHERE} initial conditions did not cause this, as we have noted that \small{SPHERE} runs exhibit the same trend. This trend towards a shallower slope despite more-realistic physics has continued in the latest version of \small{STARFORGE} (Grudi\'{c} et al., in prep.), and may point towards important missing ingredient in star formation simulations that may no longer be neglected.

No direct observations are available for the full IMF in high-density, low-metallicity conditions similar to \small{M2e4\_R1\_Z0.01}: galaxies with such low metallicities and high ISM pressures are not found in the local Universe where it is possible to resolve indiviual stars, and would have existed predominantly in the early Universe when galactic gas fractions were generally greater and metallicities were lower. The closest available comparisons would to present-day ultra-faint dwarfs (UFD) or globular clusters (GC), whose stellar populations are very old and metal-poor. In the case of globular clusters, \citet{sollima:2017.gc.massfunc} noted a strong correlation between the present-day mass-function slope and the number of relaxation times the cluster has existed for, indicating that dynamical evolution must be accounted for to infer the {\it initial} mass function. Forward-modeling GC evolution, \citet{baumgardt:2023.gc.imf} inferred $\Gamma_{\rm low}=0.7$ for $M<0.4M_\odot$ and $\Gamma_{\rm Med}=-0.65$ for $0.4<M<M_\odot$, or alternatively for a log-normal model $m_{\rm c}=0.43M_\odot$ and $\sigma =0.28 \,\rm dex$, both reasonable close to our lower-$Z$, high-$\Sigma$ models. On the other hand, they favor $\Gamma_{\rm high}=-1.3$, significantly steeper than the high-mass slope of those models.

% Comparing with the surviving $\lesssim 0.8 M_\odot$ stars in globular clusters, \small{M2e4\_R1\_Z0.1} and \small{M2e4\_R1\_Z0.01} both have IMF slopes in good agreement with the globular clusters M10, M22, and M55 \citep{piotto:1999.globular.cluster.imf}.  the general finding $\Gamma \gtrsim 0$ in 35 globular clusters analyzed in \citep{sollima:2017.gc.massfunc}. On the other hand, they are significantly shallower than the IMFs in UFDs \citep{filion:2022.ufd,filion:2024.ufd.imf}.

Overall, our model that agrees best with available observations is the high-density, Solar-metallicity \texttt{M2e4\_R1\_Z1}, whose shallow high-mass slope agrees with observations of the most extreme star clusters in the Local Group. When the \small{STARFORGE} results disagree with observations, they tend to err in the direction of an IMF slope that is too shallow, potentially hinting at neglected physics processes that are required to encourage fragmentation or regulate stellar accretion.

\subsection{The maximum stellar mass}
In \citet{grudic:2023.does.god.play.dice} we first demonstrated that \small{STARFORGE} simulations generally predict a maximum stellar mass that varies with the properties of the natal cloud, and that this is set by feedback. On more general grounds, we argued analytically that more-massive and denser clouds can tolerate the presence of more-massive stars before being disrupted, for a variety of feedback mechanisms. As long as accretion can proceed unhindered, extreme clouds should form extreme stars.

This expectation is borne out in our high-$\Sigma$ \texttt{M2e4\_R1} runs, and through direct experiments we have demonstrated that $M_{\rm max}$ is sensitive to the strength of radiation pressure and stellar winds in particular. Our fiducial physics model predics $M_{\rm max}$ consistent with the most massive known stars in the Local Group at $Z_\odot$. And at low $Z$, where these forces are much weaker, accretion can continue unabated up to much greater stellar masses.

These pre-supernova feedback processes are neglected in the \citet{padoan_2019_massive_sf} inertial-inflow (II) model, which predicts $M_{\rm max}$ as a function of the local turbulence conditions within a galaxy and the timescale for supernova feedback. Because of this, we expect that the II $M_{\rm max}$ may represent an upper bound on the mass that can be fed to a single star by turbulent eddies. We anticipate that $M_{\rm max}$ could decrease significantly if feedback were accounted for fully and this accretion flow were eventually cut off by it. Despite this, we fully acknowledge that unlike \citet{padoan_2019_massive_sf} our isolated GMC models do not encompass the $100 \rm pc+$ galactic turbulent injection scale, so we have effectively ignored the effects of the turbulent eddies driven from larger scales. $M_{\rm max}$ should be ultimately be revisited with a model that accounts for both the relevant scales and the key physics.

We also note that the scenario for EMS formation presented by \citet{gieles:2025.inertial.inflow.ems} based upon the II model is in reasonable {\it qualitative} agreement with our model. They argue that higher-$\Sigma$ conditions give rise to high accretion rates, so the stellar mass must can be reached for stellar wind feedback to compete with collapse and accretion increases with increasing $\Sigma$ and decreasing $Z$. 

There are some key differences however: in the II scenario, a large cluster mass is required for a large $M_{\rm max}$, whereas our model produces several EMS in a cluster that is not even $10^4 M_\odot$. Also, the criterion used by \citet{gieles:2025.inertial.inflow.ems} for the end of EMS accretion was $\dot{M}_{\rm wind}+\dot{M}_{\rm accretion} = 0$, but in our simulations the mechanical effect of the wind feedback disrupts the accretion flow long before $\dot{M}_{\rm wind}$ becomes comparable to $\dot{M}_{\rm acc}$ (Figure \ref{fig:ems_accretion_history}).

\subsection{Implications for models of the IMF}

An key, fundamental finding of our IMF results is that {\bf the IMF does not scale self-similarly according to a single mass parameter}. We see independent variation in both the shape of the IMF and the ratio of $M_{\rm max}/m_{\rm c}$. $m_{\rm c}$ and $M_{\rm max}$ are set by different physics: the former by fragmentation, and the latter by accretion and stellar feedback, and in general these may vary independently. The picture presented by the simulations is not consistent with e.g. gravoturbulent theories of the IMF that predict a straightforward scaling the overall distribution according to the sonic mass \citep{hopkins:2013.imf.variation,chabrier_hennebelle_2014} or a more generically temperature-dependent self-similar scaling \citep{jermyn:2018.temp.dept.imf}. 

Another important takeaway for IMF models is the general lack of significant variation in the high-mass slope. Quite often the high-mass slope is taken to be the varied parameter studies investigating IMF variation, often while fixing $M_{\rm max}$ to a standard value such as $100-150M_\odot$. Our results indicate that the opposite may occur in nature: $M_{\rm max}$ varies while $\Gamma$ stays relatively fixed, so it is arguably more physically-motivated to vary $M_{\rm max}$. For the purposes of modeling e.g. photometry or radiative feedback $\Gamma$ and $M_{\rm max}$ are practically degenerate \citep{menon:2024.imf.feedback}, but varying $M_{\rm max}$ and $\Gamma$ would generally lead to very different stellar evolution outcomes, supernova and stellar wind energetics and nucleosynthetic yields, and star cluster dynamical evolution \citep[e.g.][]{weatherford:2021.cluster.imf}. It has historically been less convenient to vary $M_{\rm max}$ due to limitations in the range of stellar evolution grids, but the situation has improved in recent years \citep[e.g.][]{boost.tracks}.



\subsection{EMS formation through accretion: key uncertainties}
\label{sec:ems_formation_details}
The formation of extremely massive stars in \texttt{M2e4\_R1\_Z0.01} is striking. The physical picture is clear: runaway accretion can proceed unabated until stellar feedback intervenes, and its ability to do so is inherently limited at high $\Sigma$ and low $Z$ (\citealt{grudic:2023.does.god.play.dice}, \S\ref{sec:mmax_experiments}).

We believe this is plausible, but caution that there are many details in this process that we have neglected, and our result should be considered provisional. First, we remind the reader of the emerging, unresolved tensions between the IMF predictions of \texttt{STARFORGE} and observations (\S\ref{sec:imf.comparison}), and pending their resolution any predictions for stellar populations should be taken cautiously. But we note here several outstanding details in this physical picture that certainly require further investigation before a reliable prediction can be made.

\subsubsection{Stellar coalescence}
In sufficiently-dense star clusters with no gas whatsoever, EMS have been shown to form readily due to purely stellar dynamical processes: massive stars mass-segregate, collide, and coalesce if the timescale is shorter than the few-Myr lifetime of VMS \citep{portegies_zwart_1999_runaway_mergers,portegies-zwart:2002,reinoso:2018.primordial.cluster.collisions,reinoso:2020.star.cluster.runaway.collisions,alister-seguel:2020.smbh.seeds.pop3.collisions,vergara:2021.pop3.stellar.collisions,vergara:2023.runaway.collisions.nuclear.clusters,gonzalez.prieto:2024.stellar.collisions,vergara:2024.bh.formation.collisions,vergara:2025.vms.runaway.collisions}

We emphasize that the EMS formation in our simulation occurs through {\it accretion alone}, because \texttt{STARFORGE} model does not model the coalescence channel at all. Although our sink-particle model permits mergers of low-mass sinks that are likely to be numerically spurious \citep{starforge.methods}, the model does not permit high-mass stars to coalesce. Even if it did, our choice of $18\,\rm AU$ gravitational softening prevents us from following stellar dynamics down to the $\mathcal{O}\left(10\rm R_\odot\right)$ pericenters at which massive stars can collide. 

Given the efficiency with which the EMS capture each other and harden in the gas-rich environment, it is plausible that coalescences {\it would} occur in our model if implemented. As the stars approach each other while accreting rapidly, determining the actual cross-section for coalescence would require knowledge of the protostars' radii, our next issue.

\subsubsection{Protostellar evolution}
\label{sec:protostellar.evol}
To determine the feedback properties of the EMS in our simulations such as protostellar radius, luminosity, and effective temperature, we use a sub-grid protostellar evolution model adapted from \citep{Offner_2009_radiative_sim}, using the calibration that reproduces the grid of detailed calculations by \citet{hosokawa:2009.protostellar.evol}. This grid goes up to $100M_\odot$ and $10^{-3}M_\odot\,\rm yr^{-1}$, which we exceed here by orders of magnitude (Fig. \ref{fig:ems_accretion_history}).

Our model generally predicts that the stars gain most of their mass while on the main sequence. However, studies have found that very-massive, rapidly-accreting stars may not contract to the main sequence at all, instead developing a bloated envelope thousands of $\rm AU$ in size \citep{hosokawa:2012.protostellar.evol}. If this were to occur, it would likely favour the formation of even more-massive stars: the bloated stars would have dramatically-reduced nuclear- and accretion-powered feedback, and would be orders-of-magnitude more likely to coalesce.

\subsubsection{Stellar winds}
\label{sec:stellarwinds}
In \texttt{M2e4\_R1\_Z0.01}, stellar winds are ultimately responsible for determining the end-point of the runaway accretion that produces the EMS. If the winds were more powerful, accretion would cease sooner, and conversely weaker winds would permit more accretion. The actual parameters of the winds from such exotic stars are highly uncertain, as we must extrapolate an order of magnitude in mass and metallicity from the regimes where existing prescriptions have been tested observationally \citep{sabhahit:2022.vms.mass.loss}. Another concern is the validity of using a single-star evolutionary track, given that a significant fraction of massive stars are known to be in a binary configuration close enough to affect stellar evolution \citep{sana:2012.binary.evol}.

Even if we knew the exact mass-loss rate that a given EMS should have, to na\"{i}vely couple that mass and energy in the present STARFORGE feedback framework would not be self-consistent: our model operator-splits gas accretion and feedback injection, but each of those operations invokes a contradictory sub-grid description of the circumstellar flow. If EMS really do accrete rapidly while also injecting winds, it would not occur as a simple, spherically-symmetric, steady flow: mass would have to come in through a disk or filaments, while simultaneously being launched in a wind through diffuse cavities by radiative or magnetocentrifugal acceleration. Such a complex flow would not be modeled by our current sub-grid models.

These questions, combined with those of protostellar evolution (\S\ref{sec:protostellar.evol}), highlight the need for more-detailed, self-consistent models of the flow around a massive protostar, resolving down to the protostellar radius while capturing the geometry and intermittency of the accretion flow that would occur in a realistic environment.

\subsection{EMS formation: implications}
\label{sec:ems_implications}
Assuming EMS {\it do} form in nature in a manner consistent with our simulations, there are several important implications for stellar and black hole populations.

\subsubsection{Formation of massive black holes}
The EMS are well above the $50-130M_\odot$ initial mass range for stars expected to end their lives in a pair-instability supernova \citep{fowler:1964.pair.instability,rakavy:1967.pair.instability.stellar,barkat:1967.pair.instability.supernova,fraley:1968.pair.instability.supernova,heger:2002.pop3.nucleosynthesis,woosley:2007.pulsational.pair.instability,farmer:2019.pair.instability.bh.gap}. Instead, they would collapse directly to a massive black hole, with the precise mass subject to uncertainties about mass-loss discussed about \ref{sec:stellarwinds}.

The conditions simulated in \texttt{M2e4\_R1\_Z0.01} are representative of the predominant high-pressure, low-metallicity conditions of the early Universe, so it would be fairly generic for star clusters to form and host massive black holes. Increasing the initial black hole mass by an order-of-magnitude compared to a standard IMF would accelerate the timescale for the light-seed channel for supermassive black holes, potentially resolving some of its difficulties accounting for the early emergence of supermassive black holes at high redshift.

\subsubsection{Stellar wind pollution}
The efficiency of stellar wind pollution scales extremely steeply with stellar mass, e.g. the yield of a $200 M_\odot$ star is thought to be 100 times greater than that of a $50 M_\odot$ star \citep{higgins:2023.stellar.wind.yields,higgins:2025.stellar.wind.yields}. Thus, winds from VMS or EMS may contribute to the abundance patterns of globular clusters and high-redshift starburst galaxies \citep{prantzos:2006.gc.enrichment, bastian:2018.gc.abundances,pascale:2023.godzilla,senchyna:2024.gnz11, gieles:2025.inertial.inflow.ems, shi:2026.vms.pollution}.

On the other hand, for wind pollution to work, the wind material {\it has to get into the stars}, which it most certainly does not in our simulation: once the wind bubble breaks out, it violently disrupts the cloud and halts star formation altogether. For an in-situ polluter to work, the energy of the polluting wind must be small enough that it is contained within the protocluster while star formation is ongoing. Alternatively, the energy of the wind would have to be {\it dissipated} efficiently: the transport of energy in a stellar wind bubble is governed by the turbulent mixing of hot and cold gas \citep{lancaster:2021.wind.sims}, and numerical convergence on this problem has not been achieved because the cooling length-scale on which the turbulent mixing occurs is not resolvable in current global simulations \citep{lancaster:2024.wind.bubbles}, including our own. Thus, the actual the dynamical effect of stellar wind feedback as a whole remains admittedly uncertain.

\subsubsection{Photometry and SEDs of stellar populations}
While they live, the EMS dominate the bolometric luminosity of the star cluster, and the bolometric light-to-mass ratio is an order-of-magnitude greater than the typical $\sim 10^3 L_\odot M_\odot^{-1}$ from a young stellar population with a standard IMF (\citealt{starburst99}, c.f. Table \ref{table:integrated_props}). This would have clear observational implications for the inference of stellar population properties from integrated light, which typically does assume a standard IMF (an assumption often required to break degeneracy; \citealt{ashworth:2017.cluster.photometry.degeneracy}). Effective temperature scales only weakly with stellar mass, so the {\it color} of a young star cluster with EMS would not be substantially different from a cluster with a standard IMF. Thus, the photometry of the young cluster would be likely be well-fit by a star cluster with an order-of-magnitude greater mass, and other derived properties such as mass density would be overestimated in turn.

Assuming the EMS have reached the main sequence, most of their excess light would be in the UV. The stellar lifetime decreases only weakly with stellar mass at these very high masses, so the {\it integrated} UV over the lifetime of the stellar population would also be greatly enhanced compared to a standard IMF. Consequently, the $L_{\rm UV}/\rm SFR$ conversion factor would be significantly elevated, and the star formation {\it rate} of a galaxy producing EMS would be overestimated. This is one of several possible explanations for the tension between models and observations of the rest-frame UV luminosity function at high redshift \citep{harikane:2023.uvlf}. Others include a scaling in star formation efficiency \citep{somerville:2025.hiz.sfe} (also demonstrated in our simulations) and bursty star formation \citep{sun:2023.bursty.sf}.

Lastly, the formation of EMS or more generally a top-heavy IMF at low-metallicity, high-redshift conditions is consistent with the model of \citet{cameron:2023.jwst.topheavy.imf}, who found that an excess of massive stars (or equivalently, the presence of excessively-massive stars) could account for the spectral energy distribution of a low-metallicity Ly$\alpha$-emitting galaxy at $z\sim 6$.


\subsubsection{Stellar feedback energetics}
EMS would dominate the overall feedback budget from their star cluster in terms of radiation and winds. They would {\it not} contribute additional supernovae, because we expect them to collapse directly to black hole. However, if these stars shed their mass as winds at a terminal velocity comparable to their escape speed ($\sim 3000 \rm \,km\,s^{-1}$), so the overall energetics are essentially the same as if they {\it did} explode. For an order-of-magnitude comparison: a typical IMF produces roughly 1 supernova per $100M_\odot$, so a specific feedback energy of $\sim 10^49\,\mathrm{erg}\,M_\odot^{-1}$. The \texttt{M2e4\_R1\_Z0.01} cluster has roughly half its mass in EMS, and if half that mass were to be shed in $3000\rm km\, s^{-1}$ winds then the specific wind energy of the cluster would be $2\times 10^{49} \,\mathrm{erg}\,M_\odot^{-1}$: wind feedback from this cluster would be comparable to, if not dominant over, its supernova feedback.

The effect of an exotic and top-heavy IMF on {\it radiative} feedback was studied in detail in numerical experiments by \citet{menon:2024.imf.feedback}, who found that the dynamical effect of the additional feedback was not large at high $\Sigma$,because it remained in a regime where gravity still dominated over feedback. But to our knowledge, the effect of the massive amount of prompt wind feedback seen in our simulation has {\it not} been explored, and may contribute to the regulation of galactic-scale star formation and wind launching at high redshift.


\section{Conclusion}

In this work we have presented the first parameter study of \small{STARFORGE} radiation-MHD star formation simulations to covers 2 orders of magnitude in both surface density and metallicity, exploring the resulting variations in star formation outcomes in extreme environments while accounting for most key physical processes. Our findings are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
	\item We have confirmed that the star formation efficiency on the scale of an individual GMC is expected to scale to high ($\gtrsim 20\%$) values at high surface density (Fig. \ref{fig:sfe}), {\it whilst accounting for all stellar feedback processes acting in concert from individual stars}. At high $\Sigma$, the SFE is also sensitive to metallicity because it determines the strength of stellar winds and the abundance of dust coupled to radiation pressure. SFE scales less steeply with $\Sigma$ than prior models without a self-consistent IMF: $\rm SFE \propto \Sigma^{0.4}$ for small $\Sigma$ instead of $\propto \Sigma$ \citep[e.g.][]{fall:2010.sf.eff.vs.surfacedensity,grudic:2016.sfe}. This happens because more-massive stars populate the high-mass tail at higher surface densities, increasing the specific rates of feedback energy and momentum injection.
	\item We find general variation in the IMF as a function of both surface density and metallicity (Table \ref{table:imf_params}). The behaviour of the \citet{chabrier_imf}-parametrized peak mass $m_{\rm c}$ is nonlinear: at Solar metallicity, it increases with surface density, but at 1\% Solar metallicity it decreases. In certain areas the simulations do a good job of reproducing the observed IMF, but some possible tensions have emerged between the simulated and observed IMF (\S\ref{sec:imf.comparison}), particularly the absence of high-mass slopes at least as steep as \citet{salpeter_slope}.
	\item The high-mass cutoff the IMF also varies with $Z$ in a manner that depends upon $\Sigma$. At low $\Sigma$, it is relatively insensitive to $Z$, but at high $\Sigma$ much more-massive stars can form if metallicity is low, due to much-weaker winds and radiation pressure. Overall, we anticipate variation in $M_{\rm max}$ due to the varying ability of stellar feedback to regulate runaway accretion depending on environement \citep[e.g.][]{grudic:2023.does.god.play.dice}. Variation in the high-mass slope $\Gamma$ is relatively small in comparison.
	\item We have disentangled some of the processes responsible for regulating the maximum stellar mass in extreme conditions. At Solar metallicity, both winds and infrared radiation pressure on dust grains are both individually important. Without these forces, runaway accretion can proceed unabated to much higher masses.
	\item In our highest-$\Sigma$, lowest-$Z$ run, we find an exotic formation mode for extremely massive ($10^3 M_\odot$+) stars. Four stars in the center of the cluster come to dominate the overall accretion rate, with $\dot{M} \propto M^{2/3}$ (Fig. \ref{fig:ems_accretion_rate}), quantitatively consistent with competitive accretion models \citep{bonnell:2001.competitive.accretion}. The 4 VMS pair up hierarchically into a quadruple system, which continues to accrete until stellar wind feedback finally disrupts the cloud violently. The resulting IMF is best described by 2 components consisting of the primary EMS system plus the rest of the star cluster. This process has many uncertainties (\S\ref{sec:ems_formation_details}), but if realized in nature it may have profound implications (\S\ref{sec:ems_implications}).
\end{itemize}

\section{IMF fitting with Hamiltonian Monte Carlo sampling}
\label{appendix:mcmc}

Throughout this study we describe the simulated IMFs in terms of the Bayesian posterior distribution of the parameters of the IMF distribution function the stellar masses were likely to be drawn from. Specifically, we compute the Bayesian log-likelihood

\begin{equation}
	\ln \mathcal{L} = \ln p\left(\mathbf{\Theta}\right)+\sum_i \ln \phi \left(\log m_i; \mathbf{\Theta}\right),
\end{equation}

where $p$ is the prior and $\phi \left(\log m_i; \mathbf{\Theta}\right)$ is the marginal likelihood of drawing (log-) mass $\log m_i$ given IMF parameters $\mathbf{\Theta}$.

For the parameters we adopt the following broad, uniform priors: $\log m_{\rm c} \in [-2, 2]$, $\ln\sigma \in [-2, 2]$, $\Gamma \in [-10, 2]$, $\log m_{\min} \in [-4, 4]$, $\log m_{\max} \in [-4, 4]$. For all IMF samples the posteriors end up are much narrower than these intervals, so the choice of prior does not affect any statistical conclusions made in this work.
